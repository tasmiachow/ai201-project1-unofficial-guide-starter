I admit I was surprised when the idea of a friendly joust over the respective merits of Central Park and Prospect Park was first proposed to us. It had never occurred to me that anyone seriously considered the two parks comparable. I have nothing against Prospect Park. I have actually been to Prospect Park. It is, as I recall, in Brooklyn. I am sure that if I found myself near it again and had nothing better to do, I would be happy to watch the grass grow, or whatever it is people do there. But seriously. I look forward to sharing with you why Central Park is not only the most visited, most famous, most beloved, but also  wait, I’m going to need the caps lock button for this  THE GREATEST URBAN PARK ON EARTH.

To: Vaux Populi

Subject: Blushing Horses

I’m sure Frederick Law Olmsted and Calvert Vaux, the architects of both these parks, would be surprised by your surprise, as Prospect was their clear favorite. It’s widely understood among landscape architects that Central Park was a mere practice run, and Prospect Park the true masterpiece  like God making man before woman.

I didn’t know Manhattanites actually used Central Park. It’s a wonder you’re even able to, since it’s so entirely overrun by tourists. Though I did once ford a treacherous river of Lycra-clad bikers to find a small flat spot of grass where Upper East Siders sardine together in search of the perfect tan. Ah, park as annex to gym and tanning salon! Inspired!

Consider, by way of contrast, our Long Meadow: the pastoral ideal at its finest, with undulating hills dotted with ancient trees, among which a diverse array of humanity frolics with happy abandon. The difference between our parks is that mine is full of New Yorkers in their own gorgeous backyard, and yours is full of suburbanites snapping pictures out of pedicabs.

I’ve got nothing against tourists. I just don’t want to be around a lot of them all at once, especially when I’m trying to relax. But they do have the darnedest accoutrements  their horse carriages, for example. Everyone on them seems so embarrassed. Even the horses are mortified.

From: Vaux Populi

To: A Little Bit Country

Subject: Blackberry Picking

You are right: Central Park is often crowded with tourists, particularly the park’s southern acreage. The Isle of Innisfree it is not.

But I like the crowds, and I suspect Olmsted and Vaux would, too. Great numbers of people from different backgrounds and economic classes commingle outdoors amid the “harmonizing and refining influence,” as Olmsted put it, of trees, grass and water. Somehow, with more than 35 million visits a year, it works. The strolling pedestrians, the Lycra-clad bikers, the horse carriages, those pedicabs backed up near the statue of Balto  the greatness of Central Park is that it accommodates all of this traffic and remains inviting and rejuvenating. This owes a lot to Olmsted and Vaux’s decision to ban the geometric tyranny of the street grid from the park  nary a right angle is to be found within its borders  and their use of topography to disperse the crowds among verdant hills and dales.

There are many good reasons so many people come to Central Park rather than, say, Prospect Park. I could catalog them  the plush sward of Sheep Meadow, the boat pond, the elegant bridges  but let me suggest the Mall alone as reason enough. This is one of the finest esplanades in the world, passing under a cathedral-like canopy of American elms (the park contains one of the last great surviving stands of these excellent trees) and ending at Bethesda Terrace. If there is anything in Prospect Park  or any other urban park  that can rival that, I’d like to see it.

Since you mention crowds, I also want to draw your attention to the park’s less visited northern region, where the Old World pastoral aesthetic of the southern park gives way to a more rough-hewn American ideal, inspired by those Hudson River School paintings that hang in the Metropolitan Museum of Art. I’m talking about the park that lies beyond the museum, beyond the tall pines that sway above the Great Lawn. A pasture of wildflowers descends into a deep and forbidding forest. Nearby are the bushes where my wife picks blackberries in the summer (and once caught a wicked case of poison ivy), and farther down the road primordial chunks of schist jut from the ground. Like Prospect Park, Central Park is almost entirely man-made, but here is a hint of unbridled wildness.




Subject: Bowl of Light

I suppose I’ll give you the Mall  though it stretches for only a few hundred feet, and seriously undermines your right-angles claim. But the reason we have nothing like your Mall is because it would be entirely out of place.

Which brings us to design.

This is where Prospect Park clearly outshines its unfortunate forebear. It’s not Central Park’s fault, the poor dear; its construction was fraught with obstacles. Start with an emaciated rectangle of big rocks and bad soil. (The very topsoil was shipped in from New Jersey!) Block out two unmovable reservoirs, and slash it into bits with four major transverse roads. Mix in a corrupt, incompetent, penny-pinching oversight committee that so exasperated the architects that they resigned in disgust before the park’s completion. Et voilà! A valiant though hopelessly compromised effort that would prove extremely useful in preparing for their magnum opus across the river.

A frustrated Olmsted wrote, “I should like to show you what I really am and could do with ... a moderate degree of freedom from the necessity of accommodating myself to infernal scoundrels.”

He had his chance a year later. Brooklyn’s plot was broad, had excellent soil, and was unencumbered by reservoirs or roads. The generosity of the land was matched by the graciousness of the park’s commissioner, who scrupulously protected the integrity of their designs. The result was a park with a coherence that was so sadly lacking in the mash-up they’d recently abandoned.

Prospect Park is divided into thirds: a gracious meadow, an immense lake, a lush native forest. Grass. Water. Trees. The trinity of benevolent nature. Each is a marvel  and each is bigger and better than its Central Park counterpart.

The meadow stretches for a full mile, and seems even longer, boomeranging around to give the illusion of limitless distance. (Olmsted understood that glimpses are often better than a straightforward view.) It’s surrounded by massive planted berms that block the sights and sounds of the city and create what the writer Tony Hiss terms a “bowl of light.”

The park’s watercourse is an exquisite catalog of the many forms water can take. It begins life as a spring, pours into two tranquil ponds that skirt the meadow, then becomes an ebullient woodland stream. After a series of dramatic waterfalls, it matures into a winding river flanked by tall cypresses and sweet gums, and finally flows into a 60-acre lake, home to more than 20 species of fish.

This is a real lake, mind you, with reeds and muck and waves lapping at a sinuous shore, not a fenced-off, concrete-bottomed reservoir. You can fish, feed the ducks, skip stones, get your feet wet. You can play fetch with your dog in an upstream pond. Most important, you can gaze at the lake and think thoughts generated only by large bodies of water.

As for the forest, go there when it’s hot  the temperature is 15 degrees cooler. Your breathing will change. You’ll find yourself speaking in hushed tones. The sounds are all birdsong and water, and the air is thick with the scent of lindens. At 146 acres, the forest is so vast you may get lost. Modeled on the Adirondacks wilderness, it’s full of centuries-old oaks and centered on a precipitous 100-foot ravine. (But your park has gorges, right? Those blaring transverse roads?)

While I willingly concede that size is not everything (Prospect Park is smaller than Central Park, for instance), in the case of the uninterrupted sweep of a meadow, a lake, a wood  well, bigger is obviously better. Not even the birds like a patchy forest.

From: Vaux Populi

To: A Little Bit Country

Subject: Love on Bow Bridge

I think you are confusing ease of creation with success of design. I don’t doubt that Central Park was a more challenging endeavor than Prospect Park, where the land was cheaper, the earth more pliable, and the politics less bumptious. After finishing Central Park, Olmsted and Vaux could have designed Prospect Park in their sleep. In fact, I wonder if they were not half asleep when they did design it, given its soporific prettiness. (My memory of the Long Meadow is that it looked like a nice place to take a nap.) Like tennis with the net down, easier is not necessarily better.

Those transverse roads you disdain are a good demonstration of the designers’ ingenuity in meeting a practical challenge. One early proposal for the park dispensed with crosstown roads, on the assumption that the east and west sides would have even less to do with each other than Manhattan and Brooklyn. Other plans put the roads on the surface for all to see. Olmsted and Vaux kept the roads but sank them from view, so that you nearly have to walk right up to them to know they are there. Landscape architecture meets urban planning. Problem solved.

As for the reservoirs in the middle of the park, they posed another design obstruction, but no contemporary New Yorker will regret them.

One was later drained and turned into that scrumptious carpet of blue and rye grass known as the Great Lawn. The other, the Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis Reservoir, is a 106-acre breeze-generating expanse of clean fresh water, by far the largest body of water in either of our parks. It is even more spectacular now that the chain-link eyesore that used to surround it has been replaced by a low black steel and cast-iron fence. In spring, the shoreline cherry trees explode with blossoms finer than those around the Tidal Basin in Washington.

Reservoirs, rocks and “infernal scoundrels” may have all upset Olmsted’s vision, but, as he learned, integrity of design is a concept that probably has more currency in Brooklyn coffeehouses than on Manhattan streets, where it has been abused for centuries and not always for the worse. Olmsted surely rolled over in his grave when Robert Moses was named parks commissioner in 1934 and immediately added many playgrounds, a dozen ball fields and an enlarged zoo. The fact is, though, Moses made it a better park.

Moses’s additions speak both to the multiplicity tucked into the park’s contours  it seems larger than 840 acres  and to its ever-evolving vibrancy. The park is a sanctuary, but there is nothing sanctified about it. Instead of reverential tableaux we get the pageant of life: skaters busting moves near the Mall, Shakespeare lovers lining up at the Delacorte, Beatles fans communing with John Lennon in Strawberry Fields, children sailing remote-controlled boats on the Conservatory Water, and blissed-out romantics on Bow Bridge. All of this is embraced by the Central Park Drive, a rolling, curving 6.1-mile circuit that strings together the jewels.

My one serious complaint: automobiles. Olmsted would be horrified, and I’m with him (and against Moses) on this one. Automobiles have nearly 500 miles of roadway in Manhattan; do they really need our 6.1 miles, too?

From: A Little Bit Country

To: Vaux Populi

Subject: We Happy Few

You can have your Moses additions. We’ve got them, too, mostly on the perimeter, and I don’t like them. There’s enough stuff in cities. Parks are places you go to get away from stuff. (His band shell is an exception  summer concerts are always a delight. Our parks both have stellar programming, but at Prospect’s concerts, you needn’t arrive at sunrise to secure a spot.)

But a nap on the Long Meadow? What a lovely idea! Before you doze off, though, let’s have a look around.

There’s a golden ratio of space to people in Prospect Park: It’s not so packed that you can’t get a sense of being alone with your thoughts, yet full enough to enable some superb people-watching.

And oh, the humanity. You might see some pickup soccer between Hasidim and Haitians. Park Slope tweens flying kites alongside bearded Pakistani men. Girls in vintage dresses, reading. Trinidadians playing cricket. Hipsters making up games with giant red balls. A Salvadoran family clustered around a smoking grill. (Cooking outside, though one of life’s great pleasures, is forbidden in Central Park.)

And yes, lots of people napping  Luxuriant stretch! Yawn!  because the scene imparts a deep sense of ease.

So I’ll gladly give you “most famous,” and please do take “most visited.” Judging from the vitriolic online forums, your 35 million is a tight fit.

From: A Little Bit Country

To: Vaux Populi

Subject: Agreed

You’re welcome to nap in our paradise. Walk through Endale Arch into the Long Meadow, and you’ll find that the city has miraculously disappeared.

The trouble with Central Park is that you can never forget where you are. Your reverie is constantly shattered by luxury high-rises lowering at every horizon. And yes, your treeline-dwarfed-by-skyline is a favorite photographic cliché, but to my mind it bears an unseemly resemblance to a real estate ad.

“A man’s eyes cannot be as much occupied as they are in large cities by artificial things ... without a harmful effect,” Olmsted wrote.

Maybe it all depends whether your ideal park visit is like a day in the country or a day in a museum. Ultimately, Vaux Populi, I suppose the city needs both.
